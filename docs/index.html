01 · The question

The fear is measurable. Is the geography?

Women's experiences in public space differ from men's, and the disparity shows up in survey after survey. In a 2022 UK national survey, 32% of women reported feeling unsafe in public spaces after dark (Elkin 2022). Across 28 global cities, women were about 10 percentage points more likely than men to feel unsafe in metro systems (Ouali et al. 2020). Fear of violence limits confidence, mobility, and social life (Koskela 1997), and that fear is spatially patterned (Valentine 1989): women manage risk by avoiding "dangerous places" at "dangerous times," so public space becomes temporally segregated.

Whether that fear tracks the actual spatial distribution of offenses — and whether infrastructure conditions in public space are levers on that harm — are empirical questions. This analysis examines every public-space incident with a recorded person victim in NYPD complaint data from 2021 to 2025, joins it with six infrastructure indicators (streetlight outages, pavement condition, sidewalk defects, subway distance, vacant land, abandoned buildings) and Census context, and disaggregates by victim gender.

Three questions structure what follows: where do incidents concentrate; which conditions correlate with that concentration once spatial dependence is dealt with; and does darkness itself — measured through 145,598 streetlight outage records — change incident counts within a tract.

02 · Where it happens

One connected corridor, from the South Bronx to Midtown

Incidents cluster hard

Public-space incidents concentrate in Midtown Manhattan and extend into Harlem and the South Bronx, with secondary clusters in Downtown Brooklyn, Jamaica Center, Flushing's Main Street, and Coney Island's commercial strip.

Global Moran's I = 0.510, p < 0.001

368 hot spots, 502 cold spots

Getis-Ord Gi* separates the city into statistically hot and cold terrain. Hot spots trace Manhattan's commercial spine; cold spots sit in Staten Island, southern Brooklyn, and eastern Queens.

Getis-Ord Gi*, tract level

The clusters are connected, not scattered

Local Moran statistics show the High-High group as a single connected region running down Manhattan's commercial areas. The Low-Low group is low-density, car-oriented, and far from the subway. The few outliers are isolated nodes in quiet surroundings.

03 · Who, and when

The gendered part is the clock, not the map

Assault is the largest offense category (23.5%), followed by harassment (20.3%); sex crimes and rape together are 2.2% of incidents. But victim sex and timing vary sharply by offense type — and the most severe, most female-victim offenses are the ones concentrated in darkness.

Rape: 98% female victims, 67% in darkness. The highest darkness share of any offense type. Harassment and kidnapping, by contrast, are daytime offenses.

The pattern holds over time as well as across offenses. Incidents rise every spring and peak from late May to early October — and the whole seasonal profile has shifted upward year over year (seasonal Mann-Kendall τ = 0.650, p < 0.001).

04 · What correlates

Streetlights and subways, once space is handled honestly

Raw correlations here mislead: outage counts rise with street length and tract area, and renter share is entangled with subway distance and poverty. A negative binomial model handles the overdispersion (variance-to-mean ratio: 166), but its residuals stay spatially autocorrelated — neighboring tracts still move together. Adding a Markov random field smooth over the tract adjacency graph removes that dependence entirely and lifts deviance explained to 73.9%.

What survives the correction:

0.65 subway distance IRR / SD — the largest association in the model 1.20 streetlight outages IRR / SD — the largest positive one 0.89 sidewalk defects IRR / SD — negative, against the disorder reading

Deterioration indicators built from 311 complaints — sidewalk defects, vacant buildings, vacant land — come out negative or null after adjustment. Complaint data registers reporting propensity as much as physical condition: conditional on everything else, a high complaint volume can mark a monitored, civically engaged place rather than a neglected one.

And the model refit on female-victim incidents, with the resident female population as exposure, returns nearly identical coefficients — the largest difference across eight predictors is 0.027. The environmental correlates of women's risk are the correlates of everyone's risk. The geography is shared.

05 · The causal test

Turn off the lights, and… nothing detectable happens

The cross-section says darker tracts record more incidents. But does darkness itself do the work? A fixed-effects Poisson panel — 2,228 tracts × 59 months, each outage weighted by the days it stays open — asks whether a tract's incident count moves when its own lighting changes.

Without tract fixed effects, every darkness lag is positive and significant. With them, every own-tract coefficient collapses to zero (contemporaneous β = 0.001, p = 0.74). An event study around repairs of outages lasting 30+ days finds nothing on either side of the repair — and the design is precise enough to rule out effects larger than ±1.3% per month.

The lighting–incident link lives in persistent differences between places, not in month-to-month darkness. Consistent with the experimental literature: only high-dosage lighting interventions have moved crime (Chalfin et al. 2022), and minor outages showed no effect even at street-segment scale in Chicago (Chalfin, Kaplan & LaForest 2022).

06 · What it means

Shared geography, gendered clock

The environmental factors linked to female victims are the same as those for victims overall — so avoiding certain places only addresses part of the risk women face. What is distinctively gendered in these data is the timing and type of offense. The crimes that overwhelmingly affect women also happen most often in darkness, which means the after-dark risk — and the stakes of infrastructure that improves visibility at night — fall mainly on women.

At the same time, fixing a streetlight should not be expected to cut incident counts the next month. The panel is blunt about that. If lighting matters, it matters as part of the durable character of a place, not as a switch. Testing that further will take data on who is actually present in public space, finer spatial units, and long-run infrastructure changes beyond this panel's horizon.

Methods

Cross-section: negative binomial GAM with a Markov random field smooth over the queen-contiguity tract adjacency graph (islands patched, graph symmetrized), resident population as exposure offset. Panel: fixed-effects Poisson, 2,228 tracts × 59 months, outages weighted by open days, tract / year / calendar-month fixed effects, tract-clustered SEs. Built in R with mgcv, spdep, sf, and tidycensus.

Data: NYPD complaints, NYC 311, DOT pavement ratings, DOB complaints, PLUTO, MTA stations, ACS 2020–2024.
