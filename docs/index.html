01 · The question

The fear is measurable. Is the geography?

Women's experiences in public spaces differ from men's, and the disparity is measurable. A 2022 UK national survey found that 32% of women felt unsafe in public spaces after dark (Elkin 2022). A study of 28 global cities found that women were approximately 10% more likely than men to feel unsafe in metro systems (Ouali et al. 2020). Fear of being unsafe undermines women's confidence and limits their social lives (Koskela 1997), and this fear is spatially patterned (Valentine 1989): women frequently manage perceived risk by avoiding "dangerous places" at "dangerous times," so public space becomes temporally segregated.

Whether that fear aligns with the actual spatial distribution of offenses and harm — and whether urban infrastructure conditions in public space are levers on that harm — are empirical questions this analysis takes up. It examines public-space incidents with recorded person victims in NYPD complaint data from 2021 to 2025, disaggregated by victim gender, joined with six infrastructure indicators (streetlight outages, pavement condition, sidewalk defects, subway distance, vacant land, abandoned buildings) and ACS socioeconomic context.

Three objectives structure what follows: characterize the temporal and spatial patterns of public-space offenses over five years; estimate which infrastructure features and socioeconomic factors are associated with the concentration of offenses across tracts after correcting for spatial autocorrelation, including whether the correlations differ for female victims; and test whether the duration of damaged streetlights — 145,598 outage records from 311 — is associated with the number of incidents.

02 · Where it happens

A single connected region, from the South Bronx and Harlem to Midtown

Incidents cluster strongly

Public-space incidents concentrate in Midtown Manhattan and extend into Harlem and the South Bronx, with secondary clusters in Downtown Brooklyn, Jamaica Center and Flushing's Main Street in Queens, and Coney Island's commercial district.

Global Moran's I = 0.510, p < 0.001

368 hot spots, 502 cold spots

Getis-Ord Gi* identifies hot spots concentrated in Manhattan's commercial areas, extending into Harlem and the South Bronx, while cold spots sit in Staten Island, southern Brooklyn, and eastern Queens.

Getis-Ord Gi*, tract level

Connected, not scattered

The High-High group is a single connected region running down Manhattan's commercial areas from the South Bronx and Harlem to Midtown. The Low-Low group comprises low-density, car-oriented residential areas away from subways. The few spatial outliers are isolated nodes in quiet surroundings, or in parks and institutional areas.

03 · Who, and when

Victim sex and timing vary sharply by offense type

Assault-related offenses are the largest category of public-space incidents (23.5%), followed by harassment (20.3%); sex crimes and rape together comprise about 2.2%. But victim sex varies markedly by offense type — and the most severe and gendered harms are concentrated in darkness.

Rape: 98% female victims, 67% occurring in darkness. The offense most strongly associated with darkness, followed by robbery (56%) and motor-vehicle theft (51%). Harassment and kidnapping predominantly occur during daytime.

A consistent seasonal pattern holds over the five-year period: offenses increase in spring and peak from late May to early October, and the upward trend persists despite the seasonality. The seasonal Mann-Kendall test yields τ = 0.650 (p < 0.001) — the entire seasonal profile has shifted upward annually.

04 · What correlates

What survives spatial correction

Bivariate correlations are confounded: streetlight outages rise with street length (r = 0.62) and tract area (r = 0.52), and renter share is entangled with subway distance (r = −0.58) and poverty (r = 0.55). Tract counts are also severely overdispersed (variance-to-mean ratio: 166), and the aspatial negative binomial model leaves spatially autocorrelated residuals (Moran's I = 0.164). Adding a Markov random field smooth over the tract adjacency graph eliminates the spatial dependence (residual I = −0.034, p = 0.997) and raises deviance explained to 73.9%.

In the spatial model, per one standard deviation of each predictor:

IRR 0.65 subway distance — the largest association in magnitude IRR 1.20 streetlight outages — the largest positive association IRR 0.89 sidewalk defects — negative, as are vacant land (0.93) and vacant buildings (0.95)

Renter share (IRR 1.13), housing vacancy (1.11), and poor pavement (1.09) are also positively associated with incident rates. Indicators of deterioration are negatively associated or show no association — and since these are built from 311 complaints and administrative filings, tract-level incident counts measure reported offenses, and reporting propensity may vary across places and offense types.

The model refit on female-victim incidents, with the resident female population as exposure, returns coefficients similar in sign and magnitude — the largest difference across the eight predictors is 0.027 (sidewalk defects). The female sexual-offense model returns no coefficient distinguishable from zero: sexual offenses are about 2% of incidents, and the intervals are correspondingly wide.

05 · The panel test

Temporary darkness has no detectable effect within tracts

The cross-section says tracts with more streetlight outages record more incidents. The panel asks whether a tract's incident count moves when its own lighting changes: a fixed-effects Poisson model over 2,228 tracts × 59 months, with each outage weighted by the number of days it remains open in the month.

The pooled model, without tract fixed effects, returns positive and significant coefficients for all four own-tract darkness lags (contemporaneous β = 0.050). With tract, year, and calendar-month fixed effects, no own-tract coefficient differs from zero (contemporaneous β = 0.001, p = 0.74). An event study around closures of outages lasting at least 30 days shows no association at the lead or any of the four event-time terms (all p > 0.14) — and the design can exclude closure effects larger than approximately ±1.3% per month.

The association between streetlighting and incidents is attributable to long-term differences between tracts. Consistent with experimental evidence: a tactical high-dosage lighting intervention reduced crime in NYC public housing (Chalfin et al. 2022), while minor outages showed no effect at street-segment scale in Chicago (Chalfin, Kaplan & LaForest 2022).

06 · What it means

Shared geography, gendered timing

The environmental factors linked to female victims are the same as those for overall victims, so avoiding certain places only addresses part of the risk women face. What stands out as gendered in these data is the timing and type of offenses. The crimes that mostly affect women also happen most often in darkness — so the risk after dark, and the importance of infrastructure that improves visibility at night, fall mainly on women.

At the same time, fixing infrastructural problems should not be expected to reduce incident counts the next month. Testing these conclusions further will need data on the number of people present, more detailed spatial units for offenses, and studies of long-term infrastructure changes beyond this panel's scope.

Methods

Cross-section: negative binomial model with a Markov random field smooth over the tract adjacency graph, resident population as exposure offset (female population for female-victim models). Panel: fixed-effects Poisson, 2,228 tracts × 59 months, outages weighted by days open, with tract, year, and calendar-month fixed effects and tract-clustered standard errors. Built in R.

Data: NYPD complaints, NYC 311, DOT pavement ratings, DOB complaints via 311, PLUTO, MTA subway stations, Census ACS 2020–2024.
